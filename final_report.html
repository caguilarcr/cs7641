NBA Game Prediction Models Analysis

Introduction

Being a sports analyst requires one to efficiently predict outcomes of games and figure out which players are most likely to score high number of points, provide assists, gather rebounds, etc. in each team.

Another problem for analysts is figuring out which players to draft/trade who can positively impact the winning probabilities of one's team by gelling with the given set of players.

Problem Definition

In this report we try to predict whether a team would make it to the playoffs or not given regular season statistics

Data Collection and EDA

We had a robust data source (Basketball-Reference.com) that contained a large volume of statistics over a 24-year time span. Our initial data had 161 columns and 712 rows. Based on our knowledge of NBA, we decided to remove a few of the variables including the number of games played, number of wins/losses of a team, teams rankings based on number of wins, the team name, season, arena name, attendance, etc. as either these directly influenced whether a team made it to the playoffs/or not(e.g.: team rank, given top 8 teams based on number of wins in each conference qualify for playoffs, including it as a feature did not make sense) or were not relevant (e.g.: arena name, attendance, etc. ). Our final dataset contained 134 features, 1 response variable (Playoffs: 0 or 1) and 712 rows.

Data Description

Our dataset contains columns aggregated from 4 tables:

  • PG: Per Game Stats
  • PP: Per 100 Poss Stats
  • Adv: Advanced Stats
  • SHT: Shooting Stats

We have named the columns in the following manner: {Table}_Stats or {Table}_Opp_Stats where {Table} is one of PG, PP, Adv or SHT. Presence of Opp in the column name indicates that the corresponding statistic is for the opponent. Some of the important stats are:

Exploratory Data Analysis

We make the box plot of some of the columns and to figure out how they relate with our response variable (Playoffs):

As seen in the figure below, we notice that teams that make it to the playoffs generally make higher number of field goals (PP_FG), make higher number of free throws (PP_FTA), have a higher average age (Adv_Age), and have higher defensive rebound percentage (Adv_DRB_pct) as compared to teams that fail to make it to the playoffs. Also, teams that make it to the playoffs generally limit their opponents in Number of shots (PG_Opp_FG), Number of 2 point shots (PG_Opp_X2P), Number of Free Throws (PF_Opp_FT).

The effect of Number of 2 point shots for the team (PP_X2P) and Number of Opponent Turnovers (PG_Opp_TOV) on whether a team makes it to the playoffs or not is not very clear.

Methods

We chose to run 8 different classification models. We analyzed a gamut of algorithms with varying complexity. We wanted to compare the performance of the models taught during the class with advanced (and more complex) algorithms. We then chose top performing models and tuned hyperparameters for them. The results of the 5 algorithms for which hyperparameters were tuned are compared and results are shown in the next section.

Conclusion

After following the model selection process described in the previous section, we calculated accuracy on the test set by the trained and hyperparameter tuned Naive Bayes model. The final accuracy that we get on the test is 63.55%. Other scores are shown in the tables below

Model Accuracy AUC Recall Prec. F1
Naive Bayes 0.6355 0.6779 0.7009 0.6560 0.6777

The confusion matrix on the test set is also shown below

After comparing several models of varying complexity, we found that Naive Bayes performed the best. We could think of a couple of reasons for Naive Bayes model's best performance for our dataset

  1. The dataset that we have chosen is simple (only 5 features after PCA). Our result is in accordance with Occam's razor which states that the simplest explanation is preferable to one that is more complex. Naive Bayes is a relatively simple model with fewer assumptions and hence, works well on a simple dataset.
  2. After performing PCA, we get 5 independent predictors which satisfies Naive Bayes' assumption of feature independence.
